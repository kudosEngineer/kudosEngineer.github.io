What I love about this work

I love that threat hunting is fundamentally a thinking problem. You start with nothing — no alert, no ticket, no obvious indicator — and you follow a hypothesis through telemetry until the story either holds or falls apart. That process of chasing something invisible, building a picture from fragments of endpoint data, network flows, and deception traps, and finally surfacing something real — that's what keeps me in this field.

What excites me just as much is the other side of it: turning what you find into a detection that fires automatically the next time. Good detection engineering means the threat you hunted last week can never hide again. There's something deeply satisfying about closing that loop.

What I've worked on

Currently working as a Cybersecurity Consultant and Threat Hunter — running intel-driven hunts using Zscaler Smokescreen, CrowdStrike EDR, and Vehere PCAP, and contributing detection use cases toward an AI-powered Autonomous SOC initiative.

Previously I ran the full spectrum from SIEM onboarding and use case creation, to DLP administration, XDR automation, and quarterly red team simulations with Cymulate. That role taught me what it actually means to own security operations end-to-end, including supporting ISO 27001 (ISMS) and SEBI CSCRF audit cycles.

Early in my career I built an Azure Sentinel SIEM POC from scratch, wrote KQL detection use cases, and cut triage time by 30%. That's where I caught the detection engineering bug.

Why I write

Security knowledge has a hoarding problem. A lot of what practitioners learn — the real-world hunting techniques, the detection logic that actually works, the IR lessons from live incidents — stays locked inside company walls. I write to push back against that.

If something I've hunted, built, or broken helps someone level up, that's reason enough to write it down.
